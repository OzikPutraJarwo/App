Introduction

This page explains the mathematical formulas used to calculate various psychrometric properties in the Psychrometric-Mollier chart application. All calculations are based on standard thermodynamic principles for moist air.

Saturation Vapor Pressure (P_ws)

The saturation vapor pressure of water at a given temperature is calculated using the Magnus-Tetens approximation:

\( P_{ws} = 610.94 \exp\left( \frac{17.625 \cdot t}{t + 243.04} \right) \)

Where:

  • \( P_{ws} \): Saturation vapor pressure (Pa)
  • \( t \): Temperature (°C)

Dew Point Temperature (T_dp)

The dew point temperature is the temperature at which the air becomes saturated with water vapor. It is calculated by inverting the saturation vapor pressure formula:

\( t_{dp} = \frac{243.04 \ln\left( \frac{P_w}{610.94} \right)}{17.625 - \ln\left( \frac{P_w}{610.94} \right)} \)

Where:

  • \( t_{dp} \): Dew point temperature (°C)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)

Humidity Ratio (W)

The humidity ratio is the mass of water vapor per unit mass of dry air. It can be calculated from the partial pressure of water vapor:

\( W = \frac{0.62198 \cdot P_w}{P_{atm} - P_w} \)

Conversely, the partial pressure can be found from the humidity ratio:

\( P_w = \frac{W \cdot P_{atm}}{0.62198 + W} \)

Where:

  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg water vapor / kg dry air)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)
  • \( P_{atm} \): Atmospheric pressure (Pa)

Relative Humidity (RH)

Relative humidity is the ratio of the actual vapor pressure to the saturation vapor pressure at the same temperature:

\( RH = \frac{P_w}{P_{ws}} \times 100\% \)

Where:

  • \( RH \): Relative humidity (%)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)
  • \( P_{ws} \): Saturation vapor pressure at dry bulb temperature (Pa)

Enthalpy (h)

The enthalpy of moist air is the sum of the enthalpy of dry air and the enthalpy of water vapor:

\( h = 1.006 \cdot t + W \cdot (2501 + 1.86 \cdot t) \)

Where:

  • \( h \): Enthalpy (kJ/kg dry air)
  • \( t \): Dry bulb temperature (°C)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')

The humidity ratio can be solved from enthalpy:

\( W = \frac{h - 1.006 \cdot t}{2501 + 1.86 \cdot t} \)

Specific Volume (v)

The specific volume of moist air is calculated using the ideal gas law for both dry air and water vapor:

\( v = \frac{287.058 \cdot (t + 273.15) \cdot (1 + 1.6078 \cdot W)}{P_{atm}} \)

Where:

  • \( v \): Specific volume (m³/kg dry air)
  • \( t \): Dry bulb temperature (°C)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( P_{atm} \): Atmospheric pressure (Pa)

The humidity ratio can be solved from specific volume:

\( W = \frac{\frac{v \cdot P_{atm}}{287.058 \cdot (t + 273.15)} - 1}{1.6078} \)

Wet Bulb Temperature (T_wb)

The wet bulb temperature is found iteratively by solving the energy balance equation for adiabatic saturation. The formula involves finding \( T_{wb} \) such that:

\( (2501 - 2.326 \cdot T_{wb}) \cdot W_s - 1.006 \cdot (t - T_{wb}) = W \cdot 2501 \)

Where:

  • \( T_{wb} \): Wet bulb temperature (°C)
  • \( t \): Dry bulb temperature (°C)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( W_s \): Saturation humidity ratio at \( T_{wb} \) (kg/kg')

This equation is solved numerically using iteration.

Absolute Humidity (AH)

Absolute humidity is the mass of water vapor per unit volume of moist air:

\( AH = \frac{W}{v} \times 1000 \)

Where:

  • \( AH \): Absolute humidity (g/m³)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( v \): Specific volume (m³/kg)

Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD)

Vapor pressure deficit is the difference between saturation vapor pressure and actual vapor pressure:

\( VPD = P_{ws} - P_w \)

Where:

  • \( VPD \): Vapor pressure deficit (Pa)
  • \( P_{ws} \): Saturation vapor pressure (Pa)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)

Frost Point (T_f)

For temperatures below 0°C, the frost point is calculated using the ice saturation formula:

\( t_f = \frac{272.62 \ln\left( \frac{P_w}{611.2} \right)}{22.46 - \ln\left( \frac{P_w}{611.2} \right)} \)

Where:

  • \( t_f \): Frost point temperature (°C)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)

Dry Bulb Temperature (T_db)

The dry bulb temperature is the temperature of air measured by a thermometer freely exposed to the air but shielded from radiation and moisture. It is one of the primary independent variables in psychrometric calculations.

In the psychrometric chart, the dry bulb temperature is represented on the horizontal x-axis.

Key relationships with other properties:

  • Higher T_db increases the saturation vapor pressure (\( P_{ws} \))
  • For constant relative humidity, higher T_db increases humidity ratio (W)
  • For constant humidity ratio, higher T_db decreases relative humidity (RH)

Vapor Partial Pressure (P_w)

The partial pressure of water vapor is the pressure exerted by water vapor molecules in the air mixture. It is related to the humidity ratio by:

\( P_w = \frac{W \cdot P_{atm}}{0.62198 + W} \)

Where:

  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg water vapor / kg dry air)
  • \( P_{atm} \): Atmospheric pressure (Pa)

Moisture Content (μ)

Moisture content (also known as percentage saturation) is the ratio of actual humidity ratio to the saturation humidity ratio at the same temperature:

\( \mu = \frac{W}{W_s} \times 100\% \)

Where:

  • \( \mu \): Moisture content (%)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( W_s \): Saturation humidity ratio at the same temperature (kg/kg')

Note: This differs from relative humidity which uses vapor pressures instead of humidity ratios.

Specific Heat Capacity (c_p)

The specific heat capacity of moist air is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of moist air by one degree Celsius:

\( c_p = 1.006 + 1.86 \cdot W \)

Where:

  • \( c_p \): Specific heat capacity (kJ/(kg·°C))
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')

The term 1.006 kJ/(kg·°C) is the specific heat of dry air, and 1.86 kJ/(kg·°C) is the specific heat of water vapor.

Density (ρ)

The density of moist air is the mass per unit volume. It can be calculated from the specific volume:

\( \rho = \frac{1 + W}{v} \)

Or alternatively using the ideal gas law:

\( \rho = \frac{P_{atm} - 0.378 \cdot P_w}{287.058 \cdot (t + 273.15)} \)

Where:

  • \( \rho \): Density (kg/m³)
  • \( W \): Humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( v \): Specific volume (m³/kg)
  • \( P_{atm} \): Atmospheric pressure (Pa)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)
  • \( t \): Temperature (°C)

Humidity Deficit (HD)

Humidity deficit is the difference between the saturation humidity ratio and the actual humidity ratio at the same temperature:

\( HD = W_s - W \)

Where:

  • \( HD \): Humidity deficit (kg/kg')
  • \( W_s \): Saturation humidity ratio (kg/kg')
  • \( W \): Actual humidity ratio (kg/kg')

Note: Unlike VPD which is in pressure units (Pa), humidity deficit is in mass units (kg water vapor / kg dry air).

Saturation Vapor Concentration (D_vs)

Saturation vapor concentration (also called saturation vapor density) is the mass of water vapor per unit volume at saturation conditions:

\( D_{vs} = \frac{1000 \cdot P_{ws}}{461.5 \cdot (t + 273.15)} \)

Where:

  • \( D_{vs} \): Saturation vapor concentration (g/m³)
  • \( P_{ws} \): Saturation vapor pressure (Pa)
  • \( t \): Temperature (°C)
  • 461.5: Gas constant for water vapor (J/(kg·K))

Volume Mixing Ratio (dry basis) (VMR)

The volume mixing ratio (dry basis) is the ratio of the partial pressure of water vapor to the partial pressure of dry air:

\( VMR = \frac{P_w}{P_{atm} - P_w} \times 10^6 \)

Where:

  • \( VMR \): Volume mixing ratio (ppm)
  • \( P_w \): Partial pressure of water vapor (Pa)
  • \( P_{atm} \): Atmospheric pressure (Pa)

Note: This is equivalent to the humidity ratio multiplied by the ratio of molecular weights (18.015/28.965).

Psychrometric Difference (PD)

The psychrometric difference is the difference between dry bulb temperature and wet bulb temperature:

\( PD = T_{db} - T_{wb} \)

Where:

  • \( PD \): Psychrometric difference (°C)
  • \( T_{db} \): Dry bulb temperature (°C)
  • \( T_{wb} \): Wet bulb temperature (°C)

This difference is used in psychrometer calculations and is related to the rate of evaporative cooling. A larger difference indicates drier air.
